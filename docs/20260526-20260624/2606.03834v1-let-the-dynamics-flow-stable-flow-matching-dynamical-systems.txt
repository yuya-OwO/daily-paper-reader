# Let the Dynamics Flow: Stable Flow Matching Dynamical Systems 

Rodrigo P´ erez-Dattari 1, Francisco Leiva 2, Andrea Testa 3, Leonel Rozo 4, Javier Ruiz-del-Solar 2, No´ emie Jaquier 1

Abstract —Flow matching has recently emerged as a powerful approach for imitation learning, enabling scalable, expressive, and multimodal motion policies. However, incorporating formal stability guarantees into these generative models, a prerequisite to ensure safe and generalizable robot behaviors, remains a significant challenge. While modeling robot motions as dynamical systems allows for such stability-based inductive biases, existing frameworks struggle to capture the rich action distributions inherent in complex robotic tasks. This paper introduces Stable Flow Matching Dynamical Systems (SFMDS), a novel framework that bridges the gap between high-capacity generative modeling and formal Lyapunov stability guarantees. SFMDS parametrizes dynamical systems via flow matching while simultaneously con-straining the model to a family of stable solutions. We propose two variants: a soft constraint based on a penalty term, and a hard structural constraint embedded directly in the model architecture. We further extend both formulations to Lie groups. Experiments on benchmark datasets, in simulation, and on a humanoid robot show that SFMDS learns stable, scalable, and multimodal dynamical systems in low- and high-dimensional state spaces, enabling safe and expressive robot motion generation. 

Index Terms —Flow Matching, Imitation Learning, Dynamical Systems, Lie groups, Lyapunov stability, LaSalle’s invariance principle 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Flow matching [23, 24] has recently emerged as a powerful generative model in downstream tasks due to its scalability, expressivity, and simple training procedure. In robotics, flow matching policies have demonstrated exceptional performance in synthesizing robot motion skills via imitation learning, efficiently learning complex multimodal action distributions and outperforming classical behavior cloning approaches [4, 7, 12, 41]. Despite its strengths, flow matching is not inherently designed for robot motion generation and control and thus lacks rigorous safety guarantees necessary when generating robot actions. In contrast, previous works on robot motion learning recognized the relevance of introducing stability guar-antees for controlled generalization by representing motion policies as learned dynamical systems [13, 25, 30, 32, 33, 37], thus ensuring safe, robust and generalizable robot behav-iors [2]. Related work. Khansari-Zadeh and Billard [13] pioneered time-invariant stable dynamical system learning by enforcing structural Lyapunov constraints on the parameters of systems modeled via Gaussian mixture models. To overcome the lim-ited accuracy of this model, several approaches learned Lya-punov functions to shape or correct the target dynamics [14, 22, 10, 17]. Alternatively, another line of works leveraged diffeomorphisms to transfer the stability of a simple system onto the target demonstrated dynamics [32, 33, 37, 43]. In this case, the model accuracy largely depends on the expressivity of the diffeomorphism, typically an invertible neural network. To allow for greater architectural flexibility, some methods opt for weak stability guarantees via regularization, at the expense of stable-by-design guarantees [30, 31]. In this paper, we build on the foregoing approaches and enforce stability either weakly via regularization or strictly via diffeomorphisms. While recent works learned dynamical systems via neural

networks [30, 31, 33, 37, 42, 43], they have not fully leveraged the power of generative models. Several approaches notably borrow diffeomorphic backbones from normalizing flows [28] for stability mapping [33, 37, 38, 42]. However, their dy-namics remain either deterministic or driven by low-variance unimodal noise, and therefore lack the capacity to encode com-plex, potentially multimodal behavior. In addition, normalizing flows training generally relies on explicit maximum-likelihood estimation that requires computing Jacobian determinants. Flow matching instead simplifies training via a simulation-free objective, leading to state-of-the-art results [23, 24]. Recently, S 2Diff [6] provided weak stability guarantees on diffusion processes by guiding them via learned certificates. S2Diff differs from our work as it aims to optimize control policies with respect to a given cost function, whereas we aim to learn behaviors from demonstrations. Moreover, we exploit the inherent simplicity of flow matching instead of the stochastic differential equations of diffusion to achieve both weak and strict stability. Closer to our work are stable Riemannian flow matching policies [8], which equip the flow matching vector field with stability to the support of the target behavior distribution using LaSalle’s invariance princi-ple [36]. However, this method offers no stability guarantees for the underlying robot motion dynamics. Instead, inspired by [8, 36], we employ LaSalle’s principle to constrain the solution space of flow matching models. This enables the enforcement of stability guarantees for robot trajectories while capturing complex multimodal action distributions. Robotics applications commonly require learned policies to control both end-effector position and orientation, which intrinsically belong to non-Euclidean spaces such as the Lie group SE(3) . Yet, most approaches assume Euclidean state spaces, thereby limiting their practical use. Recent works underscore the importance of geometric formulations for stable dynamical systems, representing states on Lie groups [38, 25] or on Riemannian manifolds [42]. Building on this, we design stable flow matching dynamical systems on Lie groups. 

Contributions. This paper introduces Stable Flow Matching Dynamical Systems (SFMDS) , a novel generative model that combines the scalability and expressivity of flow matching with formal stability guarantees to learn stable and expressive multimodal dynamical systems. Unlike existing approaches that rely on explicit dynamics parameterizations, SFMDS implicitly models the dynamics via flow matching (Sec. III), while constraining the learned solutions to an admissible set, defined by criteria from positive invariance and (global) asymptotic stability analysis (Sec. IV). Inspired by previous work on learning diffeomorphisms for stability [33, 38, 31], SFMDS enforces stability by integrating latent Lyapunov functions into flow matching (Sec. V). The main contributions of this paper are: (1) Soft SFMDS that weakly enforces stability via regularization; (2) Hard SFMDS that guarantees stability via structural constraints; 

(3) The extension of both soft and hard SFMDS to Lie groups, enabling stable learning of robot motions involving position and orientation dynamics; and (4) Two new bench-mark datasets. We test SFMDS on datasets on R2 and T2,and learn realistic motion skills featuring complex full-pose trajectories on SE(3) , both in simulation and with a real humanoid robot. Finally, we demonstrate SFMDS scalability on a 1682 -dimensional state space reaction–diffusion system. II. B ACKGROUND

D. Lie Groups 

In robotics, learned dynamical systems are typically re-quired to control both position and orientations. The latter are represented as elements of the space of quaternion, corre-sponding to the hypersphere S3, or of the special orthogonal group SO(3) . Both are instances of Lie group, which we introduce next. A Lie group G is a smooth manifold with a group structure. Specifically, it is endowed with a composition operation that is closed, associative, has an identity element, and holds an inverse to each element [19]. The smoothness of the manifold implies the existence of a tangent space TxX at each state 

xt ∈ X , consisting of all the derivatives of curves on the manifold at xt. A dynamical system on a Lie group X is described as a function that assigns to each xt ∈ X a velocity vector ˙xt ∈ TxX . The dynamics are therefore described in the disjoint union of all tangent spaces to the manifold, known as the tangent bundle T X . A key property of Lie groups is their so-called Lie algebra g, which is the tangent space at the group identity. Elements of the Lie algebra are converted onto elements of the group via the exponential map exp : g → G . The inverse operation is the logarithmic map log : G → g. Moreover, velocities can equivalently be represented as elements of the Lie algebra, ˙xt ∈ g, where a tangent vector expressed in the frame at xt is mapped to its representation in the frame at the identity via the adjoint 

operator Ad x : g → g, a linear map commonly written as a matrix. In the case of SE (3) , the adjoint relates the body-and spatial-frame representations of velocities. III. S TABLE FLOW MATCHING DYNAMICAL SYSTEMS

Accuracy metrics. We employ multiple distance metrics to quantify the accuracy of the SFMDS variants and considered baselines. For unimodal datasets, we report the Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE), Dynamic Time Warping Distance (DTWD) [26], and Fr´ echet Distance (FD) [11] between the demonstrations and the trajectories generated by the learned dynamics fθ . These metrics are computed by initializing fθ

from the same initial conditions as in the dataset, rolling out the dynamics for the same number of time steps, and compar-ing the resulting trajectories to the corresponding demonstra-tions, following standard practice [33, 37, 31]. For multimodal datasets, the aforementioned metrics are ill-defined due to the demonstrations’ multimodal nature. Instead, we compare the set of rollouts produced by fθ to the set of demonstrations using the Chamfer distance [1]. Given the set G, consisting of all the points in the trajectories generated from fθ , and the corresponding demonstration set D, the Chamfer distance is defined as CH( G, D ) := 1

Multiple rollouts are sampled per initial condition to compute these metrics. 

A. Benchmark Datasets in R2 and T2

We evaluate the performance of SFMDS and compare it with multiple state-of-the-art methods on three benchmark datasets: (1) LASA dataset [13], (2) LASA on T2 as a new benchmark dataset, (3) a novel multimodal dataset in R2. We sample ωt at every time step, except on the LASA dataset where we additionally consider the 1S variant which samples 

ωt only at t = 0 , as described in Sec. V-B. For the stability analysis, we simulated 900 trajectories per model to compute the percentage of unsuccessful trajectories. 11

1) LASA: The LASA dataset [13] is composed of 30 two-dimensional human handwriting motions. Each motion, cap-tured using a tablet PC, includes 7 demonstrations of a desired trajectory from different initial positions. The demonstrated trajectories exhibit convergent behavior toward an attractor, so the dataset is well-suited for assessing the stability of learned dynamical systems. We compare SFMDS with five benchmark methods: (1) CLF-DM [14], (2) Euclideanzing flows (Euc. Flows) [33], (3) a variant of Euclideanzing flows hereinafter referred to as Euc. Flows (NODE), where RealNVP is replaced by a Neural ODE (NODE) [5], (4) PUMA [31], and (5) Behavior Cloning (BC). CLF-DM is a relatively early method constrained to stricter Lyapunov functions and is therefore expected to exhibit the lowest accuracy among the compared approaches. Euclideanzing flows employs latent Lyapunov functions together with RealNVP [9] as an invertible mapping. Euc. Flows (NODE) follows the same approach as Euc. Flows, but uses Neural ODE (NODE) [5], known to be more expressive, as invertible mapping. PUMA also enforces stability using latent Lyapunov functions, but softly through a loss function. Finally, BC denotes an unconstrained flow matching dynamical system, trained without stability losses or constraints. All baselines, except BC, feature unimodal behaviors. Fig. 5 displays examples of learned soft and hard SFMDS, showing that both variants learn accurate and smooth behav-iors. The effect of the positive invariance loss in soft SFMDS is evident, as the vector field at the boundary points inward. This contrasts with hard SFMDS, where global asymptotic stability does not require this behavior. As shown in Table I, all methods achieve similar accuracy for the moderately complex motions of LASA. Moreover, SFMDS achieves competitive accuracy performance with the best-performing baselines. As expected, BC does not enforce stability, and 40% of its trajectories fail to converge to the equilibrium. In contrast, all SFMDS variants consistently converge, with the sole exception of hard SFMDS 1S using RealNVP, where a negligible fraction of trajectories (0.044% ) does not exactly reach the goal. Moreover, the Euc. Flows baselines also showcase a few cases of unsuccessful trajectories. As described in App. A, this behavior can arise in edge cases where the Jacobian 

Jψθ varies too sharply due to overfitting, making accurate simulation of the dynamics challenging, potentially leading to spurious behavior. RealNVP has been shown to be particularly prone to such effects [30, Sec. V.A]. We observe that the 1S 

and non-1S SFMDS variants show comparable performance, motivating the use of the latter, more common in practice, for the remainder of the experiments. 

2) LASA T2: We introduce the LASA T2 dataset, which follow the same structure as the original LASA dataset and is constructed by projecting the demonstrations onto a two-dimensional unit torus T2 = S1 × S 1. Fig. 6 displays exam-ples of SFMDS on T2, showing that both SFMDS variants accurately capture trajectories that span a large portion of the manifold. We compare SFMDS with baseline dynamical systems imposing asymptotic stability on Lie groups, namely LieImFlow [38] and PUMA [31]. LieImFlow extends the paradigm of latent Lyapunov–based methods with invertible networks, specifically employing NODE [5], to Lie groups, similarly to SFMDS in Sec. VI. From Table I, we observe that all benchmarks lead to a similar accuracy, while BC fails to learn stable dynamics, leading to 32% of failure cases.

C. Insertion Task on SE(3) with a Real Humanoid Robot 

We validate SFMDS on a real robotic manipulation task, where a RB-Y1 humanoid robot inserts a cylindrical object into a container, see Fig. 9. The pose trajectory of the left end-effector is learned using SFMDS on SE(3) from 15 

demonstrations with different initial poses, collected using a master arm with the same kinematic structure as the robot. The robot’s end-effector is controlled via a Cartesian impedance controller provided by the manufacturer, running at 500 Hz. At each control cycle, the desired velocity ˙xdt , generated by the learned dynamics fθ , is integrated using a single Euler step to produce the pose reference for the controller. The frequency of the control loop is limited by the inference time of the learned model. Since evaluating flow-matching models requires an integration procedure, they are inherently 13

slower than explicit models, making inference-time evaluation relevant. This situation is further exacerbated for hard SFMDS implemented via NODE, which require an additional inner integration loop at each inference step and the computation of the Jacobian. To alleviate this issue, we implemented a custom Taylor-series approximations of the Jacobian and its inverse, rather than relying on PyTorch’s implementations. Although these approximations are less precise, they are significantly faster and were empirically found to be sufficiently accurate to achieve strong performance. We tested a learned hard SFMDS from both seen and unseen initial poses and introduced perturbations during execution. Three examples are provided in Fig. 9 and detailed qualitative results are shown in the accompanying video. We observe that the learned model converges for most initial conditions, is robust to disturbances, and integrates seamlessly with standard robotic controllers in real time. A handful of failures occured due to workspace limits and self-collisions, which are not accounted for by SFMDS.

We consider the Gray-Scott reaction-diffusion system, which models the chemical reaction given by 

U + 2 V → 3VV → P

where U is consumed, V produced, and P is an inert product. This system is governed by the system of ODE 

∂u ∂t = Du∇2u − uv 2 + F (1 − u), (79) 

∂v ∂t = Dv ∇2v + uv 2 − (F + k)v, (80) where u and v are the concentrations of U and V , Du and 

Dv are their diffusion coefficients, and the parameters F and 

k control the rates at which U is fed to the system, and 

V is removed from it, respectively. This reaction-diffusion system has been studied extensively due to the complex spa-tiotemporal patterns it can exhibit [29]. In this experiment, we focus on a particular instance of this system, whose solution is characterized by spots that emerge after a perturbation and multiply until filling the available space 1. This is accomplished by setting Du = 0 .16 , D v = 0 .06 , F = 0 .034 , and k = 0 .065 .Moreover, enforcing Neumann boundary conditions and lim-iting the physical space in which the system evolves makes it reach the same steady state for different initial perturbations, which renders it a suitable testbed for assessing the stability of learned dynamical systems. As in [29], we simulate the system via forward Euler integration. The spatial 2D grid is set to 29 × 29 points, and the initial state is set to (u, v ) = (1 .0, 0.0) with a perturbation set to u = 0 .5, v = 0 .25 in a square region of size r = 7 

in the center of the grid. Each point in the grid is then further perturbed by adding to it a uniform noise sample, 

ϵ ∼ Unif ([ −s, s ]) , where s = 0 .25 . Fig. 12 show examples of trajectories obtained when simulating the reaction-diffusion system with the conditions described above, recording frames every 10 Euler integration steps. Note that the frames are unevenly spaced to highlight the fields’ evolution, which tends to be more noticeable at the beginning of the trajectories. To learn a model of this dynamical system, we construct a dataset by simulating the reaction-diffusion system and using the resulting trajectories, discarding the initial state frame. Although different trajectories may reach the steady state at a different frame number, we empirically found that simulating  

> 1Referred to as “pattern λ” by [29].

the reaction-diffusion system for 500 frames suffices to reach the fixed point consistently. We then stablish a new maximum frame number for all trajectories by retrieving the frame index at which we observe no noticeable changes in the states’ evolution. This results in a dataset of 10 trajectories, each consisting of 278 frames. We normalize the collected data via an independent min-max normalization of the concentration fields u and v to the interval [−1, 1] , using the numerical ranges that these fields take for all the simulated trajectories. The normalized fields are then stacked, making every data point in the dataset a 2 × 29 × 29 image, i.e., dim( ˙X ) = 1682 .We exploit the spatial correlation of the concentration fields by parameterizing the vector field uθ of soft and hard SFMDS with the UNet-like neural architecture [34] displayed in Fig. 13. The positional encoding module is implemented as described in [39], and its outputs are repeated and reshaped to match the expected dimension that allows its concatenation with the corresponding hτ and xt embeddings at each skip connection. The hard SFMDS sub-neural network ψθ is pa-rameterized by two Glow blocks [15] without actnorm layers. For all experiments, the parameter c is set to 32 . To compute the auxiliary loss functions of soft SFMDS, we draw (ambient space) samples from a clipped standard normal distribution, where clipping constrains the numerical values of the samples to lie within [−1, 1] . These samples contain values at the ambient space boundary since, as previously stated, a min-max normalization is performed over the reaction-diffusion trajectories used to construct the database. In addition, for the soft SFMDS variant, both the positively invariant set and asymptotic stability loss functions are computed performing pixel-wise projections, which we found to work better empir-ically. The model rollouts are constructed by conditioning the learned vector field uθ on ground-truth initial states for the first velocity prediction only. This velocity prediction is then used to predict the next state, which is then used as the new conditioning state for the following prediction. Additional examples of trajectories generated by the two SFMDS variants, starting from OOD initial states are dis-played in Fig. 14, where out of bounds pixels are represented in different colors to improve visualization. We observe that the behavior of the soft SFMDS variant is consistent with the positively invariant set constraint, as pixels remain bounded for the trajectory. In contrast, the hard SFMDS variant can generate states with pixels going out of bounds, since apositively invariant set is not enforced nor required in this case. Note that this is consistent with observations made for hard SFMDS in the benchmark datasets of Sec. VII-A1. Finally, we note that, although the evolution of pixels during the generation of trajectories is not numerically bounded for the SFMDS, the steady state is consistently reached by both SFMDS variants given a sufficient prediction horizon. 19
