## Abstract 

Scientific discovery via symbolic regression is often viewed as statistically and computation-ally intractable because the hypothesis space of expressions grows combinatorially with depth. This paper revisits the statistical side through the lens of PAC learning, focusing on composi-tional function trees built from a finite vocabulary of smooth operators (e.g., {+, ×, sin , exp } and affine maps). We prove that the relevant general-ization quantity—Rademacher complexity, hence the excess risk—does not necessarily blow up exponentially with the number of distinct sym-bolic structures, but is controlled by (i) the depth 

d and (ii) the Lipschitz constants of the base op-erators along the composed computation graph. Concretely, under mild Lipschitz conditions on operators and bounded affine leaves, a finite-union bound over a vocabulary of size K=|H base | to-gether with Maurer-type vector contraction yields

While deep neural networks can approxi-mate complex functions, they typically do not yield concise symbolic laws, raising concerns in the sciences where in-terpretability, extrapolation, and falsifiability are central. Symbolic regression (SR) addresses this by searching over expressions built from a vocabulary of primitives, and has led to influential systems and benchmarks (Koza, 1992; Schmidt & Lipson, 2009a;b; Udrescu & Tegmark, 2020; Cranmer, 2023; La Cava et al., 2021). However, SR is often described as “unlearnable” because the number of candidate expressions grows exponentially with depth, and finding the best expression is NP-hard in many formulations. This view conflates computational hardness with statistical learnability. PAC learning theory (Valiant, 1984) separates these issues: a hypothesis class can be PAC learnable (small sample complexity) even if optimizing over it is computationally challenging. We formalize this separation for a compositional hypothesis space motivated by scientific discovery. Our core message is that statistical generalization of compositional operator trees is governed by compositional stability (Lipschitzness) rather than by the raw count of symbolic structures. Techni-cally, we control generalization via Rademacher complexity (Bartlett & Mendelson, 2003; Mohri et al., 2018), extending classical contraction arguments (Ledoux & Talagrand, 1991) to tree-structured compositions. Discrete search procedures incur computational costs pro-portional to the number of structures explored (Koza, 1992; Petersen et al., 2021), whereas uniform convergence con-trols how reliably empirical risk ranks hypotheses once attention is restricted to a class Hd

## 2. Related Work 

Symbolic regression and scientific discovery. Classic SR is commonly instantiated via genetic programming (Koza, 1992) or program synthesis-style search, with mod-ern toolchains emphasizing physics heuristics (Udrescu & Tegmark, 2020), large-scale benchmarking (La Cava et al., 2021), and differentiable relaxations (Cranmer et al., 2020; Cranmer, 2023). Recent work has also explored deep rein-forcement learning and risk-seeking objectives for expres-sion discovery (Petersen et al., 2021), as well as broader surveys of SR methodology and applications (Makke & Chawla, 2024). In parallel, sparse discovery methods such as SINDy (Brunton et al., 2016) target dynamical systems by searching over a pre-specified library with sparsity penal-ties. Our focus differs: we do not propose a new SR search algorithm; instead, we give a statistical learnability analysis of compositional operator classes and validate the predicted scaling empirically. 

Generalization via complexity and stability. 

Rademacher complexity and related uniform conver-gence tools are standard in learning theory (Vapnik, 1998; Bartlett & Mendelson, 2003; Shalev-Shwartz & Ben-David, 2014; Mohri et al., 2018). For deep neural networks, stability and Lipschitz-like quantities (e.g., spectral norms) play central roles in generalization bounds (Bartlett et al., 2017). Our analysis is closest in spirit to compositional complexity controls (e.g., chain rules for Gaussian/Rademacher processes and vector contraction inequalities) (Ledoux & Talagrand, 1991; Talagrand, 2014; van der Vaart & Wellner, 1996; Maurer, 2016a;b), specialized here to operator trees commonly used in scientific modeling. 

Depth versus width. Much neural-network theory em-phasizes architectural depth jointly with width, norms, or margin quantities (Bartlett et al., 2017; Neyshabur et al., 2017). Here depth enters explicitly through repeated com-position of a fixed vocabulary of interpretable operators and produces multiplicative Lipschitz accumulation along trees rather than implicit coupling through parameters. This dis-tinction clarifies why symbolic/compositional models admit bounds stated directly in terms of (d, L ) without appealing to implicit layers or infinite-width limits. 

Refined contraction and localization. Recent refine-ments of Talagrand-type contraction for structured vector-valued maps sharpen dependence on norms and layer geom-etry beyond the textbook lemma (Truong, 2022). Parallel lo-calization tools—covering-number-based local Rademacher complexities (Lei et al., 2016) and offset/local frameworks that yield estimator-aware excess-risk bounds (Kanade et al., 2024)—provide routes toward sharper or hypothesis-dependent guarantees than the uniform bounds emphasized here. 

Discrete syntax graphs versus continuous estimation. 

Much SR methodology emphasizes discrete structure dis-covery combined with numerical tuning (Koza, 1992; Udrescu & Tegmark, 2020; Cranmer, 2023). From a learning-theoretic viewpoint it is helpful to separate (i) the outer discrete optimization problem—often akin to program synthesis (Petersen et al., 2021)—from (ii) the inner regres-sion problem induced once an architecture family is fixed. Our complexity estimates speak primarily to (ii): depth enters compositionally through gradients/Lipschitz maps rather than through enumeration over exponentially many graphs.

The key tech-nical tool is a tree-structured contraction inequality, con-ceptually extending Ledoux–Talagrand contraction (Ledoux & Talagrand, 1991) and compositional Rademacher results (Maurer, 2016a) to our setting.

Interpretation. The key point is that depth affects sam-ple complexity through multiplicative stability growth— equivalently (Kb √2 L)2( d−1) when arity and vocabulary are tracked explicitly (Equation (14))—rather than through the (typically much larger) number of distinct symbolic trees of depth d. Cardinality versus complexity. Let Td denote the finite collection of distinct shapes of binary trees of depth d. Uni-form convergence requires controlling complexity over the union of realizable functions associated with each shape 

after accounting for continuous parameterizations at leaves. Naively counting |T d| suggests exponential growth in dis-crete syntax; however, at each composition level Lemma 4.6 replaces the supremum over the finite vocabulary Hbase by a sum over at most K=|H base | fixed-operator subclasses, and our bounds isolate the contribution that arises purely from repeated composition along paths. Thus statistical complexity need not track worst-case enumeration when stability constants remain moderate—precisely the regime motivated by physics modeling where operators act locally smoothly on bounded domains.

Limitations. Our empirical study is deliberately con-trolled: inputs are one-dimensional and uniformly bounded, targets lie in a realizability-aligned architecture family (cor-rect depth and operator set), and distribution shift is absent. The estimator bL measures stability of the trained map on in-distribution inputs rather than certifying a worst-case global Lipschitz constant over all structured hypotheses at depth d; for compositions involving exp( ·), local slopes can grow rapidly with learned ranges, so bL need not reflect uniform worst-case operator stability without additional en-velope constraints or interval arithmetic. Finally, uniform bounds such as Equation (15) can be loose compared with hypothesis-dependent bounds when stronger structural pri-ors are available (McAllester, 1999; Maurer & Pontil, 2011). 

## 6. Conclusion 

We provided a PAC learning perspective on scientific discov-ery with compositional function trees. Despite the combina-torial explosion of symbolic structures, the statistical gener-alization of depth-d compositional hypotheses is controlled by stability: Lipschitz constants accumulate multiplicatively along paths with an explicit (Kb √2 L)d−1 prefactor on 

Rn(H1comp ) (Equation (14)), yielding excess-risk scalings of order O(Ld/√n) when K, b are treated as O(1) . Our experiments with differentiable operator trees confirm that the generalization gap tracks the predicted complexity term. These results support the view that, for stable operator vo-cabularies and modest depth, scientific discovery from small datasets is statistically feasible; the remaining challenge is computational search and inductive bias design. ## Impact Statement 

This work gives learning-theoretic foundations for composi-tional symbolic models: stability and depth shape statistical complexity in ways that complement purely combinatorial views of expression search. The emphasis on contraction along fixed trees clarifies that bounded-depth estimation with stable primitives need not inherit worst-case syntax-counting intuitions often cited against symbolic regression. The perspective is complementary to algorithm design: it does not prescribe a particular symbolic search procedure, but clarifies the statistical scaling of nonlinear coefficient estimation once a vocabulary and depth budget are fixed. Epistemic risks include trusting compact formulas under dis-tribution shift, misspecification, or confounding; our guaran-tees do not address causal correctness or out-of-distribution validity. Faster automated hypothesis discovery may also compress publication and decision timelines unless paired with scrutiny suited to the stakes. Openly stating assumed covariate ranges and validating extrapolation beyond train-ing coverage—especially when nonlinear primitives am-plify slopes—offers a lightweight mitigation aligned with the paper’s local stability viewpoint. Responsible deploy-ment should pair such tools with domain theory, uncertainty quantification, replication, and appropriate governance in high-stakes settings.
