Abstract 

We prove new necessary and sufficient conditions for uniform asymptotic stability under arbitrary switching of two-dimensional switched homogeneous systems with a finite number of subsystems using a worst-case switching analysis. The novelty of our approach is in its explicit nature, which allows us to then study in detail the codimension-one bifurcations of stability of the origin in switched linear systems and further conclude new local and global stability results for certain classes of nonlinear switched systems. In particular, we formulate an analogue of Lyapunov’s indirect method for C1 switched nonlinear systems and derive a new method for determining the existence of a bounded basin of attraction for a class of switched nonlinear systems. 

Keywords: switched systems, hybrid systems, switched dynamics, stability theory, Lyapunov stability 

1. Introduction 

Dynamical models of real-word systems are commonly assumed to either be purely continuous (as in ordinary or partial differential equations) or purely discrete (as in difference equations). However, many systems in the real world are most accurately described by hybrid models which incorporate interactions between continuous and discrete dynamics. Legged robotic motion ([37, 44]), traffic control systems ([21, 27, 46]), complex manufacturing systems ([34, 41]), and automatic transmissions in automobiles ([10]) are all examples which give rise to hybrid behaviour (see also [5, 9, 17]). Such hybrid behaviour can be modelled through the framework of switched dynamical systems ([17, 24]). Switched systems are given by a collection of continuous dynamical systems represented by autonomous ordinary differential equations (ODEs) and a time-dependent “switching signal” controlling which ODE governs the dynamics at a given moment in time. More formally, a family of functions fp : Rn → Rn, where 

p ∈ P for some finite indexing set P, gives rise to a collection of subsystems 

˙x = fp(x), p ∈ P .

A switching signal is defined to be a piecewise-constant function σ : R≥0 → P which is everywhere continuous from the right and with left limits existing everywhere ([24]) such that card (σ−1(p) ∩ J) is finite for every bounded interval J and p ∈ P . This assumption is required to avoid “Zeno-like” behaviour where we have an infinite number of switches in a finite amount of time ([24, 43]). Such behaviour is usually considered an undesirable modelling artifact of hybrid dynamical systems ([17]), so this assumption does not greatly

Much work has been done to study stability properties of switched linear and homogeneous systems, which generally falls into the two approaches: providing sufficient conditions for stability which are easier to verify, or providing necessary and sufficient conditions for stability which are harder (and often intractable) to verify. For switched linear systems, examples of the first approach include conditions related to the triangular structure of the matrices ([12, 32]) and Lie algebraic conditions ([1, 2, 25, 38]) for switched linear systems, and dwell-time conditions ([18, 33, 3]), and methods involving linear matrix inequalities to determine existence of a common quadratic Lyapunov function for the switched system ([39, 40, 47]) for both switched linear and homogeneous systems. An overview of stability methods of this type for switched linear systems may be found in the excellent survey [26] and in parts of the textbooks [24], [42], and more recently [11]. Explicit necessary and sufficient conditions for uniform asymptotic stability of two-dimensional switched linear and homogeneous systems have appeared in the literature since at least the 1980s with the work of Filippov ([14]) in the context of differential inclusions. However, the proof of sufficiency and necessity in this paper does not provide insight into how the trajectories of the switched system change qualitatively between stable and unstable regimes, which limits of possibility of bifurcation analysis and further investigation of stability behaviour for nonlinear switched systems. Other conditions proposed by Pyatnitsky and Rapoport in [36] are more transparent and applicable to bifurcation analysis, however the conditions are checked by solving a nonlinear equation in multiple unknown variables which is difficult to do in practice. A number of results in the literature (for example [6, 7, 35]) focus on the case of switching between two second-order linear or homogeneous subsystems, but their methods do not readily generalize to switched systems consisting of n > 2 subsystems. The papers [20] and [45] provide necessary and sufficient conditions for uniform asymptotic stability of a certain class of planar switched homogeneous and linear systems consisting of n subsystems. The first paper uses generalized first integrals ([30]), and the second paper considers “worst-case” switching behaviour on regions of state space (which is similar to our approach; see [29] for a general survey of stability analysis of switched systems using worst-case switching laws) and works in polar coordinates. However, in both papers there is an additional assumption (Assumption 1 in [20] and Assumption 3.3 in [45]) which restricts the class of switched systems they consider. Although this assumption may be treated in a separate case, it provides an artificial roadblock to a unified study of bifurcations of these systems. The necessary and sufficient conditions proved in this paper are valid for switched homogeneous and linear systems under the mild assumptions that the functions fp defining the subsystems are C1 in the homogeneous case, and that the switching signal is not “Zeno-like” as defined above. Our conditions also admit a pleasing graph-theoretic interpretation which makes them easier to interpret and visualize. Under the non-Zeno assumption, all of the existing works above outlining necessary and sufficient conditions for uniform asymptotic stability are special cases of Theorem 3.5 and Corollary 4.5 in this paper. In addition to this stability result for switched homogeneous systems, the explicit approach we use in the proof of the result (that is, working closer to the actual trajectories of the switched system) allows us to draw further qualitative conclusions about how stability is lost in the system, and also allows us to extend our methods to certain classes of nonlinear systems (in contrast to the similar approaches used in [20] and [45]). Theorem 4.2 provides an easily-checked test for the definitive presence or lack of local uniform asymptotic stability for C1 switched nonlinear systems which admit a common equilibrium point at the origin, in the spirit of Lyapunov’s indirect method in systems theory ([23]), and proves that there must always exist a periodic solution of the nonlinear switched system arbitrarily close to the origin if certain conditions do not hold. Theorem 4.3 yields similar results about the existence of a bounded basin of attraction for a more specific class of switched nonlinear systems. 2Although bifurcations have been studied extensively in piecewise-smooth dynamical systems ([13, 15]), little progress has been made in this direction for switched systems with arbitrary switching. We hope to remedy this situation with Theorem 4.1, which gives a classification of codimension-one bifurcations of stability of the origin in switched linear systems. Again, our explicit framework is crucial for us to be able to make the qualitative assertions in Theorem 4.1 of exactly how uniform asymptotic stability is lost in the switched system; this level of analysis could not be achieved with the more high-level approaches of [20] and [45]. Additionally, Theorem 4.3 yields some partial results which may help to classify bifurcations of the existence of a bounded basin of attraction for certain classes of switched nonlinear systems. The outline for the rest of the paper is as follows. We go over the preliminary assumptions and derive some groundwork results in Section 2, namely, we classify zero sets of quadratic forms x⊤Ax for Hurwitz matrices A and then show that we may without loss of generality impose some additional simplifying assumptions to the matrices defining our switched system, which greatly simplifies the number of cases we need to consider in the stability analysis later in the paper. Section 3.1 is devoted to proving a preliminary version of the main theorem concerning necessary and sufficient stability conditions for switched linear systems under some additional technical assumptions. Briefly, this is done by first defining a fixed number of rays S[i], i ∈ { 1, . . . , n }, and showing that in the “worst-case” behaviour the trajectories of the switched system may only switch subsystems upon crossing one of these rays. Then, having essentially reduced the problem to checking a finite number of possible “worst” switching combinations, Theorem 3.4 concludes with the main necessary and sufficient stability conditions of this subsection. In Section 3.2 we extend this result to the case of general switched linear systems by considering an augmented version of the original switched system to which Theorem 3.4 applies, and then showing that stability properties of the two switched systems are equivalent; the culmination of the work in this subsection is Theorem 3.5. Section 4 discusses some of the useful applications of Theorem 3.5: we classify the codimension-one bifurcations of uniform asymptotic stability of the origin of switched linear systems and formulate theorems which provide easily-checked conditions for stability of the origin and the existence of a bounded basin of attraction for certain classes of switched nonlinear systems. Examples are provided to illustrate the usefulness and elegance of these results. We conclude this section by extending our stability results from switched linear systems to switched homogeneous systems. Section 5 summarizes. For ease of readability, the proof of Theorem 3.4 and relevant preliminary results are moved to Appendix A, and the proof of Theorem 3.5 and relevant preliminary results are moved to Appendix B. 

2. Preliminaries 

2.1. Preliminary assumptions and results 

This paper concerns stability and bifurcation properties of switched linear and homogeneous time-invariant, continuous-time dynamical systems in R2. That is, for x(t) ∈ R2, we consider the switched linear system 

˙x(t) = Aσ(t)x(t), σ : R≥0 → P , (2.1) and the switched homogeneous system 

˙x(t) = fσ(t)(x(t)) , σ : R≥0 → P , (2.2) where x(0) ∈ R2, Ap is a real-valued 2 × 2 matrix for p ∈ P , fp is a C1 homogeneous function of fixed degree 

β, and σ is a switching signal as defined in Section 1. Recall that fp being homogeneous of degree β means that fp(cx 1, cx 2) = cβ f (x1, x 2) for all c ∈ R, p ∈ P , and x1, x 2 ∈ R.Since each fp is C1 in both the linear and homogeneous cases, we have existence and uniqueness of solutions of the unswitched subsystems ([19], §17). We assume throughout the paper that the indexing set 

P is finite, for example, P = {1, . . . , m } without loss of generality, and that Ap̸ = Aq and fp̸ = fq for p̸ = q.We will be primarily concerned with local and global uniform asymptotic stability of switched linear and homogeneous systems ([24]). We note that a necessary condition for uniform asymptotic stability is that each subsystem is asymptotically stable, since otherwise we can take the constant switching signal corresponding 3to a subsystem which is not asymptotically stable. In particular, for the linear switched system (2.1) this means we require each matrix Ap for p ∈ P to be Hurwitz, that is, to have eigenvalues with negative real parts. As shown in [4], linear switched systems admit an equivalence between many of the common notions of stability, including uniform asymptotic stability. In particular, they are all equivalent to the notion of 

attractivity . The system (2.1) is attractive if for every solution x(t) of this system,

So, a sufficient and necessary condition for a lack of uniform asymptotic stability of (2.1) is the existence of some σ : R≥0 → P and an initial value ξ ∈ R2 such that if x(t) solves (2.1) with switching signal σ and x(0) = ξ, then lim t→∞ || x(t)|| is either nonzero or does not exist. In Section 4, we will study bifurcations of uniform asymptotic stability of switched linear systems as we vary the matrices Ap defining the subsystems with respect to some external parameter. There we will consider the system 

˙x(t) = Aσ(t)(θ)x(t), σ : R≥0 → P , (2.4) where x(t) ∈ R2 and Ap(θ) is a real-valued 2 × 2 matrix for p ∈ P which varies continuously with respect to the parameter θ ∈ [α, β ] ⊆ R. A bifurcation in this context is a change in uniform asymptotic stability of the system. It is clear that the switched system (2.4) loses uniform asymptotic stability when any one of the matrices Ap(θ) ceases to be Hurwitz, so for this reason we restrict our attention to the case when the matrices Ap(θ) are Hurwitz for all p ∈ P and θ ∈ [α, β ] in Section 4.

3. Stability analysis

4.1. Analysis of bifurcations in (2.4) Using Theorem 3.5 it is now straightforward to study the codimension-one bifurcations of uniform asymp-totic stability in the switched linear systems (2.1) we have been considering thus far. For the switched system (2.4) with bifurcation parameter θ the conditions 1 and 2 of Theorem 3.5 may be used to find the bifurca-tion points in parameter space, and furthermore to identify the exact mechanisms through which uniform asymptotic stability is lost. 4.2. Some applications of Theorem 3.5 to nonlinear switched systems 

Using Theorem 3.5, it is possible to formulate conditions for local and global stability properties of particular nonlinear switched systems which can be approximated sufficiently well by linear switched systems either locally or globally. 5. Conclusion 

We developed a new approach for analyzing stability properties of planar switched linear and homoge-neous dynamical systems, which is applicable under minimal assumptions and case been explicitly worked out in the case of an arbitrary finite number of subsystems. The necessary and sufficient conditions of Theorem 3.5 and Corollary 4.5 are straightforward to verify and transparent enough to allow for a success-ful bifurcation analysis via Theorem 4.1. Theorems 4.2 and 4.3 formulate useful applications to stability properties of certain nonlinear switched systems. An intriguing future direction of study is to generalize these results to higher dimensions than two; unfortunately, as noted in Remark A.5, our approach is funda-mentally limited to two dimensions. The problem of finding necessary and sufficient stability conditions in three dimensions seems to have been first posed in [8] and some recent developments are discussed in [11], §5, wherein the authors study extremal trajectories (analogous to worst-case switching trajectories in two dimensions) with respect to a Barabanov norm associated to the matrices defining the subsystems.
